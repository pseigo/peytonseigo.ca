Bitmind - Decision Making Assistant

Monday, September 28, 2020

I built a desk­top app to help jour­nal tough de­ci­sions, pos­si­ble choices, and the con­se­quences of those choices.

This was a term pro­ject for a sec­ond year soft­ware en­gi­neer­ing course at the University of British Columbia. I wanted to cre­ate some­thing be­yond the course’s de­fault pro­ject which was a par­tially com­pleted to-do list app. The course of­fered an al­ter­na­tive: stu­dents could choose to de­sign and build their own pro­ject from scratch, as long as it ful­ﬁlled sim­i­lar cri­te­ria as the stan­dard pro­ject. Most stu­dents chose the stan­dard pro­ject: in par­tic­u­lar, 413 out of 448 (~92%) stu­dents chose the stan­dard pro­ject while the other ~8% opted for a per­sonal pro­ject.

An in­ter­est­ing do­main I wanted to ex­plore was im­prov­ing peo­ple’s lives. For this pro­ject, I wanted to help in­di­vid­u­als take re­spon­si­bil­ity in mak­ing bet­ter choices for them­selves.

The in­ter­nal ap­pli­ca­tion model is ﬁn­ished and fully tested, but the GUI was never com­pletely ﬁn­ished given the time con­straints, other courses, and life get­ting in the way. It hap­pened to be the last 80% of work for 20% of the re­turns in per­sonal growth. But alas, such is the un­for­tu­nate fate for many a side-pro­ject. That said, there is still lots to talk about and I look back fondly on the code I wrote.

The source code is avail­able on GitHub.

§ Motivation

Our ac­tions are largely dri­ven by emo­tions which are im­pul­sive and il­log­i­cal: they have no con­cept of long-term con­se­quence or risk. To act only by emo­tion is to act with­out a con­science. But, “[w]ith con­scious­ness, we can de­lib­er­ately weigh what the senses tell us, and re­spond ac­cord­ingly” (Flow, M. Csikszentmihalyi, 2008, p. 24). If one does not have strict con­trol over her mind, it can be dif­ﬁ­cult to make de­ci­sions that align with her per­sonal val­ues — es­pe­cially in high stress sit­u­a­tions.

To avoid feel­ing con­trolled by my emo­tions, I of­ten had to ne­go­ti­ate with my­self to make bet­ter de­ci­sions. If I de­cided to go with the eas­i­est or (momentarily) plea­sur­able choice, I would usu­ally re­gret it later. Procrastination was a com­mon theme.

I knew I wanted to build some­thing re­lated to men­tal health and I went through a long list be­fore com­ing up with Bitmind.

§ Project re­quire­ments

The course’s re­quire­ments for a per­sonal pro­ject were that it must:

  • Be writ­ten in Java.
  • Have model, controller, ui , and parsers pack­ages — a loose MVC struc­ture. They also wanted us to try us­ing JSON and to im­ple­ment ba­sic se­ri­al­iza­tion.
  • Use jU­nit 5 for unit tests with at least 95% code cov­er­age in the model and parsers pack­ages.
  • Adhere to the course’s check­style test.
  • Document all meth­ods with their pre­con­di­tions (“requires”), mu­ta­tions (“modiﬁes”), and pub­licly vis­i­ble side-ef­fects (“effects”).
  • Use at least one of the ob­server, com­pos­ite, or it­er­a­tor de­sign pat­terns — I used the it­er­a­tor and builder pat­terns.

They also eval­u­ated cou­pling and co­he­sion in the sys­tem, as well as uses of the sin­gle-re­spon­si­bil­ity and Liskov sub­sti­tu­tion prin­ci­ples.

§ Test-driven de­vel­op­ment

Out of every­thing in this pro­ject, I am most sat­is­ﬁed with how easy test-dri­ven de­vel­op­ment (TDD) made it to im­ple­ment the model with few bugs. It did take some time to write all the tests and doc­u­men­ta­tion, but I was able to eas­ily tweak the de­sign as I went along. Essentially, I shifted the com­plex­ity from de­bug­ging and hard think­ing to writ­ing tests and doc­u­men­ta­tion. The lat­ter is­n’t as glam­orous, but do­ing things that way can make life a lot eas­ier.

§ What I mean by “shifting com­plex­ity”

Shifting com­plex­ity from de­bug­ging to TDD is an ex­am­ple of what I meant in this tweet:

Experience teaches that “removing com­plex­ity” is of­ten just shift­ing it some­where else. https://​t.co/​5hg83l2vdd

— Peyton Seigo (@PeytonSeigo) September 5, 2020

When I wrote that how­ever, I was think­ing about the com­plex­ity that web frame­works claim to re­move with metapro­gram­ming magic. That com­plex­ity tends to reap­pear in other places, like im­pact­ing main­tain­abil­ity over the long haul and de­creas­ing the ﬂex­i­bil­ity of the frame­work. This ap­par­ent “conservation of com­plex­ity” can oc­cur for one of two rea­sons:

  1. The code un­der-the-hood is com­plex and hard to un­der­stand. A (seemingly) sim­ple high level API does­n’t dis­so­ci­ate that com­plex­ity from your ap­pli­ca­tion.
  2. Every prob­lem has a base level of com­plex­ity. “Complexity bedrock,” so to say. It is what makes the prob­lem it­self dif­ﬁ­cult and will al­ways ex­ist re­gard­less of how el­e­gant your so­lu­tion is.

In terms of ad­dress­ing this prob­lem, Daniel Berkompas sum­ma­rizes in­sights from José Valim, the cre­ator of the Elixir pro­gram­ming lan­guage, on how one should ap­proach macros. That is, when de­sign­ing a sys­tem (in Elixir), one should:

  1. Design a good, core data struc­ture for prob­lem.
  2. Design a purely func­tional so­lu­tion — a layer on top of the data struc­ture.
  3. (Optionally,) add cos­metic macros to make the func­tional so­lu­tion eas­ier to gen­er­ate.

In other words, fo­cus on writ­ing clean and ex­pres­sive code be­fore pret­ty­ing it up. This hi­er­ar­chy is great be­cause even if the macros are poorly writ­ten, a user can still use the un­der­ly­ing func­tions or data struc­tures. The Elixir guide on macros sends this home by not­ing “macros are not your API” and “the de­vel­oper plays a big role when writ­ing macros re­spon­si­bly.”

Macros should only be used as a last re­sort. Remember that ex­plicit is bet­ter than im­plicit. Clear code is bet­ter than con­cise code.

Anyways, I di­gress.

§ Key re­sults

From the start of the pro­ject, I was un­sure whether I had the cor­rect de­sign in mind for the sys­tem I was try­ing to build. I was wor­ried about mess­ing up and “designing it wrong,” but even­tu­ally had to ac­cept that there was no amount of prepa­ra­tion and plan­ning I could do to avoid that un­cer­tainty. In the end, writ­ing unit tests with TDD and vi­su­al­iz­ing how the user might use the app helped a lot with that.

Some things I did:

  • Designed, tested, and im­ple­mented an ob­jected-ori­ented sys­tem in Java from scratch (contrary to the starter code that uni­ver­sity courses of­ten give).
    • Wrote ro­bust classes by test­ing bound­ary cases and throw­ing ex­cep­tions when needed.
  • Applied the it­er­a­tor and builder de­sign pat­terns.
  • Used a sim­ple JSON li­brary to se­ri­al­ize and per­sist ap­pli­ca­tion state.
  • Practiced TDD to write ex­ten­sive JUnit 5 unit tests with greater than 95% code cov­er­age in the model.
  • Designed a GUI us­ing the JavaFX plat­form and FXML.

§ How it works

This soft­ware helps peo­ple use log­i­cal rea­son­ing for mak­ing de­ci­sions. Mark Manson ar­gues that the pur­su­ing the “path of least re­gret” is a good in­di­ca­tor for com­par­ing the im­por­tance of de­ci­sions. In other words, when one has mul­ti­ple choices, he should do what he would re­gret the least later on. It is said that the closer one is to a prob­lem, the harder it is to make an op­ti­mal de­ci­sion. The pur­pose of this ap­pli­ca­tion is to help users get their thoughts down “on pa­per” to dis­tance them­selves from ir­ra­tional emo­tions and bi­ases.

Each prob­lem to be dealt with cor­re­sponds to an en­try. A new en­try guides you through stat­ing the prob­lem and an ar­bi­trary num­ber of choices. Each choice is de­tailed with its pros and cons, po­ten­tial for long-shot suc­cess (i.e., some­thing that has a low chance for some great re­ward) and po­ten­tial re­grets for not pur­su­ing that choice. You can save your progress and come back at any point. After each choice has been ﬁlled out, the app sum­ma­rizes the de­ci­sion and choices in one view.

§ Implementation and learn­ings

§ Package struc­ture

If you want to fol­low along, you can ﬁnd the source code on GitHub. Below, we have the afore­men­tioned model, ui, and controller mod­ules for the MVC struc­ture.

There are a few other pack­ages as well that sep­a­rate out pieces of the sys­tem:

  • exceptions: Custom Exceptions and RuntimeExceptions to be more ex­pres­sive with er­rors.
  • parsers: Parsers to con­vert JSON to Java classes for each class in the model.
  • persistence: A sin­gle class, JsonEncoder, that se­ri­al­izes a class’s state as JSON.
  • util: JsonFileIO which uses the parsers and persistence mod­ules to read from and write to disk, and FxmlResourceLoader to re­duce du­pli­ca­tion in the ui.

§ System de­sign

A Journal holds Entrys that rep­re­sent prob­lems for which a de­ci­sion must be made. Each Entry has a Status and Choices, and those choices have Consequences.

§ Serialization

We were given a sim­ple JSON li­brary to use. Essentially, you have JSONArrays and JSONObjects.

A JSONObject is an un­ordered col­lec­tion of name-value pairs, where the name is a string and the value is one of Boolean, JSONArray, JSONObject, Number, String, or the JSONObject.NULL ob­ject.

A JSONArray is an or­dered list of val­ues with the same pos­si­ble types as JSONObject’s val­ues.

§ Unit test­ing

Altogether, I wrote 66 unit tests cov­er­ing the model, parsers, persistence, and util mod­ules which runs in 113 mil­lisec­onds on my ma­chine. We were re­quired to reach at least 95% code cov­er­age in the model and parsers pack­ages, so that I did.

I tested copy con­struc­tors, equals() over­rides, edge cases, and as many cases as I could come up with at each level of ab­strac­tion. Because the model stair­cases nicely from Consequences, to Choices, to Entrys, to Journals, it was easy enough to move from very spe­ciﬁc unit tests to more gen­eral func­tional-es­que tests.

The other mod­ules were mostly sep­a­rated from every­thing else, so they were easy to test in iso­la­tion.

§ GUI

The GUI main­tains ap­pli­ca­tion state via the pub­lic model APIs and (de-)serializes data when needed with the persistence and parsers mod­ules. The ac­tual in­ter­face is coded up in FXML, JavaFX’s XML-based mark-up lan­guage. The controller mod­ule is re­spon­si­ble for ma­nip­u­lat­ing the ap­pli­ca­tion state when the user in­ter­acts with the GUI.

Unfortunately, this is where I ran out of time. The trans­par­ent classes got done, yel­low is what I was work­ing on up to the dead­line, and the red is the re­main­der of what would have been im­ple­mented. Perhaps I’ll come back to it.

The ﬂow was meant to go a lit­tle like this:

  1. The home screen gives you an overview of all your en­tries. You can cre­ate new en­tries from here.
  2. On cre­at­ing a new en­try, you give it a de­scrip­tion and ﬁll out the pos­si­ble choices.
  3. Then, one by one, you can ﬁll in the con­se­quences for each choice.
  4. At the end, you are given an overview of all the pos­si­ble choices and their con­se­quences. You can make a de­ci­sion right away or de­fer it to later.

§ Takeaways

Next time, it would be nice to have more time to spend on the GUI. The per­fec­tion­ist part of me is quite neu­rotic and or­derly about these things, so it’s hard to just get ’er done slop­pily and let it be. Everything must be in its place.

With that said, I re­ally en­joyed TDD and would use it again in the fu­ture! It can feel like a slog at ﬁrst, but it pays div­i­dends if the prob­lem do­main does­n’t have too many un­knowns.
