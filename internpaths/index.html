Internpaths - Job Search App Prototype

Friday, September 4, 2020

I worked with three other de­vel­op­ers to build a soft­ware demo in less than 24 hours at the nwHacks 2019 hackathon, hosted at the University of British Columbia. At that point I was halfway through my sec­ond year of uni­ver­sity.

  • GitHub repos­i­tory
  • Project page

§ Slides

Below is the slide-deck Sergey came up with for us, and I have to say, I think our pre­sen­ta­tion went well! We had ﬁve or six judges tun­ing in at a time and ask­ing ques­tions, so that was both ex­hil­a­rat­ing and a lit­tle nerve-wreck­ing con­sid­er­ing how much we had strug­gled the pre­ced­ing 20ish hours.

§ Overview

The land­ing page was fairly sim­ple. Enter a job ti­tle and lo­ca­tion, and you’ll be di­rected to the jobs dash­board.

The dash­board shows a map with nearby job post­ings (using react-google-maps) with brief de­scrip­tions in a side­bar.

Below the map, you can ﬁnd a more ex­ten­sive list of post­ings. We burnt a lot of time at this stage trou­bleshoot­ing UI bugs and get­ting the front-end to grab data from the Mongo data­base. Unfortunately, we did­n’t have time to im­ple­ment the rest of our fea­tures. Time was short so we started work­ing on the demo. One or two of us also slept at some point.

§ Background

This is­n’t the ﬁrst time I’ve wanted to build an ag­gre­ga­tion app. Job post­ings, events, ho­tels, and ﬂights all suf­fer the same prob­lem: con­tent is split across dozens of ser­vices that do the same thing. If you re­ally want to see what’s out there, you have to sift through many plat­forms. A prim­i­tive so­lu­tion is to keep a note or spread­sheet with all the in­ter­est­ing con­tent you ﬁnd, but this can be la­bo­ri­ous and eas­ily be­come out­dated. This hackathon was prob­a­bly my ﬁrst at­tempt at solv­ing this prob­lem, and I can bet it won’t be the last.

We built a pro­to­type for a web app for ag­gre­gat­ing and track­ing the sta­tus of job ap­pli­ca­tions. For each job post­ing, you can track the stage in the ap­pli­ca­tion process, con­tact in­for­ma­tion, emails, cover let­ters, and re­sumes.

Some other fea­tures we had in mind were to:

  • Send re­minders to fol­low-up af­ter in­ter­views and af­ter ap­pli­ca­tions if you haven’t heard any­thing back.
  • Aggregate post­ings from pop­u­lar job boards and dis­play them on a map based on prox­im­ity.
  • Automated or “quick ap­ply” job ap­pli­ca­tions (you see this on LinkedIn and other plat­forms nowa­days).

Aggregating post­ings did­n’t work out be­cause all the APIs we wanted to use had ac­cess turn­arounds of more than one day, so we man­u­ally gen­er­ated some data for the demo.

Unfortunately we ran out of time be­fore ﬁn­ish­ing a min­i­mum vi­able prod­uct (MVP), but we had enough done to ex­plain our ideas to the judges. We were all new to web de­vel­op­ment, so our pro­ject just turned out as an in­ter­ac­tive mock-up. But it was a great in­tro­duc­tion to web de­vel­op­ment and an ex­er­cise in man­ag­ing small teams. It was also lots of fun, of course. The at­mos­phere of hun­dreds of stu­dents work­ing tire­lessly to build some­thing awe­some is un­like any­thing else.

I spent the week prior learn­ing React –- and try­ing out every free hip-hop class I could ﬁnd, as a uni­ver­sity stu­dent does –- so I’d be pre­pared for the hackathon. But I did­n’t have a team or any pro­ject ideas in mind. I went to a net­work­ing work­shop a few days be­fore the hackathon, but be­ing a bud­ding ﬁrst year, I had a hard time ﬁnd­ing my place amongst stu­dents who had full-stack apps, ma­chine learn­ing pro­jects, and in­dus­try ex­pe­ri­ence un­der their belts. But I met Jason Yue. A few days later, he in­tro­duced me to Sergey Karavaev and Daniel Gomez. If I re­call cor­rectly, we were all strangers to each other. But on January 26th, 2019 — the day of the hackathon — we be­came the “Goodfellas”. Jason was a huge help with his back­ground in the MERN JavaScript stack and I at­tribute most of our suc­cess to his de­ter­mi­na­tion, per­se­ver­ance, and pa­tience.

After set­tling on the idea, we spent about six hours get­ting our de­vel­op­ment en­vi­ron­ments set up. Yes, you read that right. Good thing the event of­fered bot­tom­less cof­fee and Guayakí Yerba Mate! Once we were on our feet, we raced to build our prod­uct as we stud­ied new con­cepts and tools just-in-time for im­ple­men­ta­tion.

I helped ﬂesh out of the idea, de­signed UI wire­frames, cre­ated the front end in­ter­face with React and Ant Design, and served as an or­ga­nizer for the team. I made sure every­one was in­volved in idea gen­er­a­tion and that we were com­mu­ni­cat­ing through­out the event: each per­son let the oth­ers know what he work­ing on, his progress, and if he needed help. We all had our eyes on the clock and made sure each task was bring­ing us closer to the ﬁ­nal demo.

§ The plan

Our plan was to:

  1. Come up with sev­eral ideas and choose the best one.
  2. Choose our tools and get every­one set up.
  3. Deﬁne key fea­tures and draw wire­frames.
  4. Build the pro­ject, in­cor­po­rate oth­ers’ changes, and con­tin­u­ally test.
  5. Identify any big prob­lems that come up and weigh the cost of ad­dress­ing them.
  6. Prepare our demo and pre­sen­ta­tion.

In a work­shop be­fore the event, I learned that in hackathons, sell­ing your idea is much more im­por­tant than the im­ple­men­ta­tion. In par­tic­u­lar, they ad­vised that an un­ﬁn­ished im­ple­men­ta­tion is OK and that mock-ups are enough, but it’s still im­por­tant to have a work­ing demo.

With that in mind, we prob­a­bly could have made nice mock-ups in some­thing like Figma for our en­tire app with plenty of time to spare. That would have made for a more sub­stan­tial demo and it would’ve let us think more about the busi­ness as­pects. But my goal was to get my hands dirty build­ing some­thing since it’s hard to ﬁnd time to do that dur­ing uni­ver­sity — or more re­al­is­ti­cally, it can be hard to jus­tify do­ing cog­ni­tively in­ten­sive hob­bies when you’re a full-time stu­dent tak­ing chal­leng­ing tech­ni­cal classes. Hackathons are a great way to for­get about the world for a week­end and get in the build­ing mind­set.

§ Wireframes

I worked with Sergey to sketch some rough wire­frames for our app. First we thought a bit about the themes around job search­ing.

“Proximity” and “utility” be­came our fo­cus with a map-based view and tool to track that sta­tus of ap­pli­ca­tions. Then we sketched what the app and its ﬂow might look like. We lim­ited our sketches to some­thing like two min­utes to make the most of our time. I don’t have copies of his draw­ings, but here’s what I came up with:

The idea is that re­turn­ing users would hit a land­ing page with re­cent ap­pli­ca­tions sum­ma­rized at the top, fol­lowed by a list of new post­ings to search through. Each post­ing would have its own page with its sta­tus, de­scrip­tion, lo­ca­tion, and any rel­e­vant doc­u­ments — these doc­u­ments could in­clude cover let­ters, re­sumes, and emails.

New users would have a dif­fer­ent land­ing page that tells them what the app is all about. The draw­ing on the right is a table of the other avail­able post­ings.

I went through a cou­ple it­er­a­tions of the dash­board. This is most sim­i­lar to what we ended up with. I apol­o­gize for the mess; ap­par­ently that’s what time­box­ing does to you!

I did an­other sketch of the land­ing page. The de­sign did­n’t change much, but it was clear now that the search form was the most im­por­tant part of this page. I looked at how the pop­u­lar job boards de­sign their search and re­al­ized they all do the same thing: key­words (e.g., job ti­tle) and lo­ca­tion. I won­dered if it was worth adding any­thing else –- in the sketch you can see I was still think­ing about prox­im­ity.

§ Implementation and learn­ings

I’ll be hon­est: I had no idea what I was do­ing. I spent a week try­ing to learn React from YouTube videos and blog posts, but it was­n’t enough to feel re­ally con­ﬁ­dent with the tool. That be­ing said, we did ﬁn­ish a (mostly) work­ing demo!

Most of my work was done in the React com­po­nents in internpaths/client/src/components/. I re­al­ize the folder struc­ture could use some work, but hey, I did­n’t know any bet­ter! I’d go through the im­ple­men­ta­tion, but none of it is too sur­pris­ing if you’ve ever used React.

We used the Ant Design com­po­nent li­brary to let us fo­cus more on the struc­ture of the site. These kinds of tools (CSS frame­works, light­weight JavaScript li­braries, com­po­nent li­braries) are great when you just need to get some­thing out there and don’t want to spend time rein­vent­ing the wheel.

On the topic of learn­ing… re­cently, about a year af­ter the hackathon, I spent a few days (re-)learning the ba­sics of React and picked up way more than I did the ﬁrst time.

First, I got a quick in­tro­duc­tion with a small React course on Pluralsight. The in­struc­tor gave some use­ful tips for peo­ple us­ing JavaScript ES6 for the ﬁrst time and sub­tleties for React new­com­ers. Then, I read the of­ﬁ­cial React “Getting Started” guide which ex­plains con­cepts con­cisely and is more com­plete than most videos out there.

If I had to give a tip to new pro­gram­mers, it would be to learn how to ﬁnd what you need on­line (hone your “Google-fu”) and get lots of prac­tice read­ing doc­u­men­ta­tion. Well-written docs (and books!) are a trea­sure trove. But tech­ni­cal read­ing can scare some peo­ple away (see: CMake doc­u­men­ta­tion) to other medi­ums.

§ What I would do dif­fer­ently

In the fu­ture, I’d hope to be com­fort­able enough with my tools to throw a small app to­gether in a short time than try to learn every­thing from scratch on the spot. Learning things prop­erly takes time, so I think hackathons are best suited for meet­ing and work­ing with new peo­ple, pro­to­typ­ing crazy/​fun ideas, net­work­ing, and let­ting your­self try some­thing dif­fer­ent.

With that said, I cer­tainly don’t re­gret at­tend­ing nwHacks be­cause it was such a great ex­pe­ri­ence. I’m glad I took the leap!
